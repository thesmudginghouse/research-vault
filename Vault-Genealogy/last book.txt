James Jacobus Jackson Jaksen (c. 1708 - c. 1750) was an early resident of the Hudson Valley whose life bridged the Dutch and English cultural shifts in colonial New York He is primarily identified in genealogical records by both his English and Dutch name variants. Genealogical Overview

• Birth: Born in 1708, with historical records primarily citing Kingston, Ulster County, or Queens County, New York.

• • Death: Died approximately 1750 in Rhinebeck, Dutchess County, New York. Family Origins: He was the son of James Jackson Sr. (1671-1735) and Rebecca Hallett. His father was a prominent Quaker figure in Flushing, Long Island, known for having a large family of approximately 20 children.

Historical and Cultural Context

Naming Conventions: The use of "Jacobus" and "Jaksen" reflects the influence of the Dutch community in the Kingston and Rhinebeck areas during the early 18th century, where English names were often adapted to Dutch phonetics and records.

Regional Significance: His residence in Rhinebeck occurred during the early development of the Rhinebeck Patent, a period when the area was being settled by Palatine Germans and established Dutch families.

• Legacy: James is often cited as a key link for descendants tracing their lineage to United Empire Loyalists (UEL) or early New York settlers. His branch of the family later migrated to various parts of New York, including Albany and the northern counties.

James Jackson Sr. (1671-1735) was a prominent colonial figure in the Hudson Valley and Long Island, recognized primarily for his large family and his role as a Quaker leader

Genealogical Summary

Birth: Born on August 31, 1671, in Hempstead, Queens (now Nassau) County, New York. Death: Died around October 15, 1735, in Flushing, Queens, New York.

Parents: He was the son of Col. John Jackson and Elizabeth Seaman, and the grandson of the immigrant Robert Jackson.

. Marriage: He married Rebecca Hallett (1675-1730) in 1694. Following her death, he remarried, which led to a conflict with the local Quaker community for marrying "too hastily".

Family and Legacy

Children: James and Rebecca had 20 children (all single births), 18 of whom were living at the time of his death. These children carried the Jackson name into New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, and several southern states.

• Public Service: He served as the Clerk of the Town of Hempstead starting in 1701. Boundary Dispute: Alongside Col. Isaac Hicks, he was chosen as a referee to resolve a

Burial

1780s

In 1783, refugees loyal to the British Crown began to land at the mouth of the Saint John River in what was then part of the Province of Nova Scotia. They were fleeing from persecution in the aftermath of the American Revolution and came from as far south as Georgia and as far north as Massachusetts. These refugees were not all of British origin, but included German, Dutch and Black Loyalists. The Black Loyalists included a number of freed slaves, but there were a small number of loyalists who brought their slaves with them to New Brunswick.

Discontentment with the government in Halifax led to the establishment of the areas north of the Bay of Fundy as the new Province of New Brunswick in 1784.

By 1785, so many refugees had landed and settled at the mouth of the Saint John River that the King granted a charter to the new City of Saint John, the first incorporated city in Canada. The capital was established at Fredericton, 114 km up the Saint John River.

Tomhannock (Pittstown) : Located in modern-day Rensselaer County, about 15-20 miles northeast of Albany. In 1783, this area was part of the Schaghticoke District of Albany County. It was a known settlement during the Revolution, often cited in military records regarding raids and troop movements.

Lachine, Quebec

: If the query refers to a location in North America, Lachine

(near Montreal) was named "La Chine" (The China) by explorers like Cavelier de La Salle, who believed the St. Lawrence River would eventually lead to China. However, by 1783, this was an established settlement rather than a river name.

1780s

In 1783, refugees loyal to the British Crown began to land at the mouth of the Saint John River in what was then part of the Province of Nova Scotia. They were fleeing from persecution in the aftermath of the American Revolution and came from as far south as Georgia and as far north as Massachusetts. These refugees were not all of British origin, but included German, Dutch and Black Loyalists. The Black Loyalists included a number of freed slaves, but there were a small number of loyalists who brought their slaves with them to New Brunswick.

Discontentment with the government in Halifax led to the establishment of the areas north of the Bay of Fundy as the new Province of New Brunswick in 1784.

By 1785, so many refugees had landed and settled at the mouth of the Saint John River that the King granted a charter to the new City of Saint John, the first incorporated city in Canada. The capital was established at Fredericton, 114 km up the Saint John River.

Tomhannock (Pittstown) : Located in modern-day Rensselaer County, about 15-20 miles northeast of Albany. In 1783, this area was part of the Schaghticoke District of Albany County. It was a known settlement during the Revolution, often cited in military records regarding raids and troop movements.

The Ardagh-to-Poplar Migration: A Story of Three Lots

For generations, the families of Sullivan, Dominy, and Driscoll lived side-by-side as tenants of the Fetherston Estate in the model village of Ardagh, County Longford. Their lives were geographically and socially intertwined, a bond that survived the Great Famine and the crossing of the Irish Sea.

The Village Row (1820-1848)

In the shadow of Ardagh House, the ancestral seat of the Fetherston baronets, these three families occupied consecutive plots along the village street. Their proximity defined their destiny:

• Lot 19: The Sullivans. The anchor of the group. It was here that Mary Sullivan was raised, eventually marrying the man two doors down.

• Lot 20: The Dominys. The "middlemen" of the row. William Dominy (b. 1803) lived here in a house with a dedicated "Office" (workshop), suggesting a family of skilled tradesmen.

• Lot 21: The Driscolls. The home of Daniel Driscoll (b. 1810). This plot was closest to the Ardagh House Demesne wall, placing the family at the heart of the estate's daily operations. The 1834 Generation

In 1834, your ancestor Daniel Driscoll was born in Lot 21. He grew up playing on the Ardagh village green with the Dominy children from Lot 20 and his Sullivan cousins from Lot 19. This "Estate Village" upbringing provided a level of stability and literacy that was rare in 19th- century Ireland

The Great Departure (c. 1848)

As the Famine tightened its grip on the Midlands, the three families made a coordinated decision. They did not leave one by one; they surrendered their leases in a block. Leaving the Fetherston Estate, they traveled as a single unit to the East End of London.

Rebuilding the Row in Poplar

By the 1851 Census, the "Three-Lot Connection" was re-established in the shadow of the West India Docks. Living on Susannah Street and Cotton Street, the families remained neighbors:

• Daniel Driscoll (b. 1834) utilized his "estate-bred" skills to become a master Shipwright. William Dominy (b. 1834) and his father continued their work as laborers alongside the Sullivans.

The families continued to act as godparents and wedding witnesses for one another, preserving the "Village Row" in the heart of industrial London.

Today, the stone cottages in Ardagh, Longford, stand as a testament to the community your ancestors left behind. Family Tragedy and Migration

The death of Mary Ann in late 1906 left William as a widower with several young children. This loss was the primary reason Daniel and William were sent to Canada as British Home Children:

1. Daniel Dominey (b. 1894): Sent to Canada shortly after his mother's death. Records from the British Home Children Registry or Library and Archives Canada often list children like Daniel being placed in Quebec or Ontario through organizations like Dr. Barnardo's.

2. William Dominey (b. 1898): Also sent to Canada between 1906 and 1910. Being only 8 years old when his mother died, he would have been a high-priority candidate for assisted emigration to a Canadian receiving home.

3. The Father: William Sr. remained in Poplar, where he appears in the 1911 Census as a widower living at 44 Byron Street with the children who were not sent away (George, Alice, Frederick, Albert, Florence, and Arthur). He passed away in 1916, during the middle of WWI
