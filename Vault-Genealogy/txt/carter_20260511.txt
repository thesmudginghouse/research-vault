Carter's the Beginning

At first when I started looking into the Carter Family Tree it was all of a mystery. No one really followed the family members back to England. George Carter came over to Canada from England. Edith followed as a war bride. That was basically it. During the war their son Ernie was serving over in England. He met some of the Carter's and Sissen's while stationed there in London. He took some pictures and did leave their names on the back of them. So that did help to some degree that there are relatives still there. But we have lost contact with them.

I have gathered a lot of information on the Carter's and Sissen's but cannot make positive identifications of my leads. Our cousin Wayne Gadula, Winnie's son, has taken on the research also now. He and I have been collaborating on our leads and hopefuls. Wayne has made great leaps and bounds into the past and now has tied in with a lot of documentation of our beginning relatives.

5th Generation

George Samuel Carter was born on August 19, 1884 in Poplar, London, England. He was baptized August 31, 1884 in the St. Augustine Church, Haggerstone, Middlesex, England. George came to Canada when he was twenty six years old. He arrived in St, Johns, New Brunswick on April 14, 1911. I looked and looked for records or a ship log book with George landing but cannot seem to find the name of ship he came over on. Herbert and his wife came over to Canada around the same time but cannot find them either. Looking into the history of that time era of 1911 there were a lot of unemployment. A lot of people were very poor. George was a carpenter by trade and he worked his way westward across Canada finding odd jobs as he went. He arrived in Saskatoon, Saskatchewan. There he found work as a carpenter. Edith Sissen came to Canada in 1912. They were married on October 7, 1912, by Reverend John Lewis in Saskatoon. It has been a mystery to us how George and Edith met. We had thought that Edith was a war bride but the war had not started. The story was that George had never met Edith as pen pals only in writing to each other.

George and Edith must have been friends in England and Edith kept correspondence with him after he came to Canada. Aunt Maude (Hanning) Edith's sister said that Edith was seeing a baker at the time but decided that George was the one she wanted to marry. George and Edith had four of their children born in Saskatoon and they lived at the address 1817 20th Street Saskatoon. Their first child was born on November 9, 1913, William George Carter. Their second child was named Ernest Alfred born January 17, 1915. George enlisted into the army on December 7, 1915.

"Lest we Forget"

The Federal government obtained a tract of land six miles long and two miles wide from the Poor- man's Indian Band. This parcel of land became known as "The Strip" and shall often be referred to as such throughout this story. It was to be used as a settlement for soldiers returning from World War 1, subject to certain conditions before they could apply and become successful applicants.

It must have been with apprehension and some consternation they came to this unknown land. They had left comrades and friends who had fallen in the far away fields of Flanders and other battle grounds. They came with a fierce determination to make a better life and a home for their families, as some already had families. They came to piece together the wool and the warp of their lives to form a complete tapestry for the future. Most came to virgin land; a few were to occupy homes built by the former inhabitants of the land.

The land obtained from the Poorman's Band was from the west side of the reservation and was appor tioned into twenty-nine farming units. It was opened for sale to settlers at the office of the Soldier's Settlement Board at Saskatoon at 2 p.m. on Tuesday. April 10, 1920. It was stated to be "a fine tract of land, ideal for mixed farming. The whole area is described as gently undulating, park land. This forms the only compact area or community in this district. End of quote (Archives, Regina). Prices varied from $2880.00 to $4440.00 for a half section. The average loan for stock and equipment was $1264.00 (maximum was $2000.00) for permanent improvement $457.00 (maximum was $1000.00). They were asked to keep $300.00 to 400,00 of their Joan as a protection against crop failure (Archives),

The settlers arrived at Raymore by C.N. train at various times of that year. Among the original Veterans who settled on the Strip for whom we have no story are: J. Brown-bachelor-perished in a fire in his home, Norman Gibby, Frederick Groves, John Hoskins, Jonason, Harold Johnson, Harold & Louise Wilson, J. 5. Herbert Carter 1920

Herbert Carter was born August 19, 1920 in Raymore. He was the first white baby born in the Flanders district. Herbert was born in a log shack which was left vacant by the previous owners. Which were native people. It was known as Lot 34-29-18 which is now belonged too Albert Baker. Bert's father George was building the new house at the time. Herbert attended the Lewiswyn school. For tree years and then the Flanders school was ready he went there. He finished his schooling in 1935. Herbert worked for several different farmers in the area.

He tried to join the army but was told that there were too many men, In January 1940. He left Raymore and went east to St. Catharines Ontario. He stayed with Dan and Agnes Dominey He worked at different jobs for a year and then went to Kirkland Lake to work in th emines. He went back to St. Catharines and acquired a job at the General Motors plant on Ontario Street. Bert received his first pay check from the company and they went on strike for six weeks. He tried again to join the army but was refused because of health reasons. In June 1942, Bert returned to Saskatchewan to work the harvest. After the harvest was done he moved to Vancouver and got a job working in a saw mill. Then he got a job working at the Boeing Aircraft. Where he met Janet Size.

Bert and Janet were married May 6, 1944. Janet Size was born on July 13, 1921 in Portland Oregon. Bert and Jan moved back to Saskatchewan in 1945 and bought the William Clancy farm. Their first child born was their daughter Sharon born in 1947. They left the farm and moved back to Vancouver where they had no luck finding a job.

In the spring, Bert and Jan moved back to Raymore. Lyle was born in 1949in Raymore. Bert started a trucking business but it only lasted to the spring of 1949. He worked at various jobs and then took on a farm in Southey. He moved the family to the farm. In July 1952, Dale was born and they stayed in Southey till 1954. They returned to Raymore where he and his brother Harold started a garage. They stayed in partnership until 1971 when Bert sold his half to Harold. He then bought the Martin Nester farm and started farming again. Janet worked for the grocery store and also clerked at the Co-op store.

Bert loved hunting and fishing. Every year he would go elk, deer and Moose hunting. When he went away he took all his toys with him. He would attach to his truck the travel trailer and attached the boat to the travel trailer. Funny site to see for us in Ontario where that wasn't allowed. Red necked style! He loved fishing. Bert took my brother Phil and I out fishing to a remote northern lake. At the time Phil and I were very knowledgeable about fishing for pickerel. Bert said to us put your tackle box away cause you are not going to catch a thing with any of that stuff. He put on a yellow jig and threw it over board the boat into the water and we drifted the shoreline. Phil and I used almost every lure in the tackle box to prove him wrong. Well after the forth pickerel in the boat that he had caught and boosted about them we closed the tackle box and asked for a yellow jig that he had offered before. He loved to joke and pull your leg if he could. Whenever he talked or started telling a story you never knew whether it was the truth a joke. Nine times out ten it was a joke he was telling. We would go and saddle up the horses and go and get them. They were always in the farthest part of the farms section of land the homestead was on. The slow walk back in was nice. Of course the girls Eileen Joyce and Esther were always eager to pull one over on Phil and myself. One day I decided that I was going to saddle my own horse, of course I always took Major the smallest one. Put the blanket on then the saddle tightened it up then the bit, the girls were hiding a little smile and I knew something was up. Walked the horse out of the barn, the girls were waiting but so was the rest of the family sitting out side on the porch. The girls were saddled and running the horses back and forward.. They were accomplished riders. Who loved entering the barrel racing contest on Raymore's Field Day competitions. I hopped into the saddle and gave Major a kick and giddy up. I went about ten feet made a little turn to show off my horsemanship while I had a audience. the saddle shifted and slide down to underbelly of the horse. Of course I fell and was the laughing stock or the day. Now I know why they give the horse a knee into their belly before tightening up the straps of the saddle. They bloat their stomachs with air before tightening up straps so when I said giddy up they let air out and guess what happens next no rider. Aunt Anne always made fresh buns almost everyday. Everything always tasted better on the farm. We always had fun and everyday there was something new to do or try.

Phil and I loved to hunt gophers. Jimmy used to take us out to other farms just to shoot. The farmers gave us boxes of 22 shells to kill them, One day Jack and were sitting on the front step there were two gophers playing a round the grain silo Jack went and got the gun. He told me to kill them. They were just playing jumping on each other like kids. I told him that I didn't want to, that I was enjoying watching them play. He told me how two gophers could multiple and chew through the silo walls and eat away his stored grain which was his savings in his bank. So I shot one and he shot the other. Another day Uncle Jack said a tom cat killed a chicken and we had to kill him. I don't know why but I thought he said a bob cat. So I was looking for a bob cat and it just happened that this cat had a stub for tail. I could of shot him a hundred times but as soon as I found out that the cat was my bob cat. I quit stalking it. It got away.

On day Joyce Eileen and I decided to go for ride on the horses. We had saddled them up and were outside the barn. Joyce and Eileen had some barrels set up and we taking turns practicing barrel racing for the up coming Sports Day Rodeo. I cannot remember how it happened but the rooster ran in front the horse I was on Major, of course, spooked her and she the horse lunged after the rooster. I did managed to hang on. Well that started the game on horseback lets chase the rooster. The horses were well adapted to round up the cows so corralling a chicken was not much of problem. It was funny watching the rooster running as fast as it could to get a way. Well, I was chasing the rooster towards the barn. But for some reason the rooster ran into the pig pen. All I heard was the rooster screaming and the pigs grunting. I got in there as fast as I could the one of sows had the rooster pinned down with foot and munching on the rooster rear end. I kept punching that pig in the face as hard as I could. It finally released the rooster enough for me to get the rooster out of the pen. Now being a city boy these sows are not the little bitty piggies we are used to seeing. Now the pig wants a piece of me because it had it's litter to protect and all that was protecting me from that five foot sow was a little wire fence a nailed to a board. It tried to hop the fence but it luckily it held and I bolted for the door fearing for my life. Of course the girls were laughing and threatening to tell there dad what happened. Boy, did I feel bad about it all. We went out for our ride. Uncle Jack was sitting on the step of the house waiting for us to to fish grooming the horses water and food. I knew that Esther probably told Jack what had happened.

The girls quietly walked past their father like they knew I was in trouble and they wanted no part of it. Jack had a coffee ready for me there. I sat down and there was a brief quietness. I didn't know how to tell him about the rooster. I didn't have to wait long for the silence to end. Uncle Jack took his pipe from his mouth and rested it on his leg, just speaking in a quiet whisper farmer joking voice. You wouldn't happen to know what happened to the rooster did ya? Cause I found him dead behind the chicken coop. I guess the expression on my face said it all. He laughed said don't worry. The rooster doesn't do anything except eat the food and that he was getting tired of him waking him up in the morning. Time for a new one.

Found memories of finishing supper sitting at the kitchen table talking and joking while they the girls washing and drying the dishes. You didn't dare go into Aunt Anne's domain of the kitchen. She spoiled us royally. You asked and she made it or got it for you but usually she asked you first. That was her kitchen. We would go outside and sit by the garden. Watch the birds and look at the flowers They loved there flower garden. We would retire into the living room as the sun started to set and have a drink. Jack and Anne would sit in their chairs. I will always remember Jack sitting in his chair reading a book or magazine would be sitting on his lap. Or that it was the little Chihuahua dog they had and his pipe in his other hand or hanging from his mouth. That dog never liked me, he would bite at me every time I went near it. Jack's other dog Spot and I were playing outside and I was playing rough with him. I came in with my hand bleeding. I guess my hand got caught in the dogs mouth and pulled it out breaking the skin on my hand. Uncle Jack seen my hand said no dog is allowed to bite on his farm. He went for the gun and I had a lot of explaining to save the dogs life. It was my fault not the dogs. But he had a double standard for that pet Mexican dog. Spot was a farm dog, who's job was to keep the coyotes and stray cats away form the livestock.

Jimmy and I got along and worked well together too. But one of my favorite times was going into town, in his yellow truck with Road Runner Sticker on the cab sides of the truck. That made it go faster he said, picking up a 6 or 12 pack of beer. Going out into the country and drinking the beer, talk and looking at the stars. That was a site to see all those stars. Sometimes we would stop and pick up Sheila. She was always fun and never a dull moment with her around.

Jack and Anne sold the farm and moved into town. Jack died January 12, 1999 in Raymore. Anne still is living in Raymore. Later the government bought the land back and they gave back to the Poormans Indian Reservation.

Ernie was sent oversees on May 1941. Agnes and Dan had a house built on Welland Avenue on the Wellandvale hill. Wellandvale was small valley which the Welland river ran through. There is a bridge over the river. It was a significant link in building the first and second building of the Welland Canal in the 1800's. There was also a mill on the other side

of the river owned by William Merritt also in the 1800's.. On July 25, 1941 Alfred Ernest Carter was born. The Hydro company bought the house on Welland Avenue. Dan and Agnes bought a house on Park Place which later was renamed Montebello Place.

While oversees Ernie worked with the engineers building roads in England for the army. He took night school courses for engineer training, welding, auto engineering and may more courses to get his mechanics license. While stationed in London, England, Ernie was lucky to visit relatives and got to know some o them very well. For majority of them never traveled to Canada or us the same. Maude Sissen Hanning was a true exception. Maude was Ernie's Mom's sister whom married an American, George Hanning, who came and lived in Toledo Ohio. Aunt Maude was a frequent and welcome visitor at our house in St Catharines.

In July 1944, He returned home from the war and stayed in the barracks in London Ontario. There He trained soldiers how to drive the heavy equipment. Ernie was looking for an apartment but they were hard to find. Ernie stayed at the Y.M.C.A and he met Betty Mcdonald, who had a friend named Mabel Finch. Mable's parents owned a house on Ontario Street in London. Betty Walker Mcdonald became a good friend of the family. Betty traveled the country something relating to religious matters. . Her husband Charlie was also a minister and lived in Nova Scotia.

The house in London was three stories and they lived on the second floor. Maxine and Ernie were asked to leave because the Finch's daughter Ethel was moving back home. Her husband was being sent oversees by the army for the re enforcement of war effort. The Finches in turn told them that they could take the dining room and front room on the main floor and make an apartment out of them. So Ernie fixed up the two rooms to live in. After a few months the Finches wanted the rooms back because they felt too crowded. They also had small children. Their next door neighbor to the Finches let Maxine and Ernie with Alfred rent the second floor of her house. Ann who owned the house lived on the main floor and her boyfriend lived on the third floor, they all had to share the same bathroom.

Ernie had bought a convertible coupe car with a rumble seat at the back. They traveled to St. Catharines a fair bit to visit Maxine's mother and dad. The convertible had Isinglass windows like plastic. It was very cold traveling in the winter time. .Some friends down the street in London worked in a factory making armatures. Ernie got a job there working on Saturdays or extra money. In fact he was offered steady employment from that company after his discharge from army. Ernie started having a lot of pain in his lower back. He went to the army hospital and had surgery on his tendons to relieve the pain. Maxine and Alf would visit Ernie in the hospital. It certainly was an eye opener to see men with so many disabilities, amputations and loss of memory because of the war.

L Ann whom owned the apartment they were living in wanted the apartment back but offered Maxine and Alfred to move downstairs to stay with her and cook meals for her. It was getting cold and winter was coming. The hospital was quite a distant from their apartment. Ernie wasn't allowed out of the hospital and Maxine was getting very lonely by herself.

Maxine decided to move to St.Catharines and stay with Her parents. She moved into the top floor of the house on Park Place. The rent payments was $12.50 every two weeks. When Ernie was able to drive, He came home on some weekends. Eventually he was released from the hospital after eight months. Ernie was discharged from the army on July 9, 1945. After being released he left London and went to St. Catharines. Ernie looked for a job but they went not too plentiful. He started making leather wallets, belts and purses.

They would drive to Toronto and pick up supplies and materials from Lewis Crafts. Ernie traded in the coupe for an old four seated car. That car had lots of fond memories of flat tires on the way to Toronto. Maxine's younger brother use to accompany them on many drives to Toronto. Bill used to tell them when they were about to get a flat tire by the blue smoke trailing the car. Ernie used to buy retread tires cause they could not afford new ones.

Maxine was working at Mrs.Hempalls Beauty Salon and she started to make jewelery from sea shells and sold them to customers at the shop. Ernie finally started working at the Royal Bank on Queen and St. Paul Streets.

Ernie bought a some land on Pelham Road lot 43. It was a skating rink next to Bill Tempest Garage. He had the basement dug out and then did the basement in blocks by himself. He built the house by himself with the occasional help from friends. Jim Brydges helped inside the house, he was a carpenter by trade. Ernie contracted the electrical wiring out. Maxine when she could helped out but one day she did helped putting up the side of the house, the next day Monday she into labour.

June was born June 2, 1947. They moved into the house in August. The inside walls of the house were up but not plastered. They finished the inside while they lived there. Ernie hired Nicholson Construction to level the ground outside the house. The ground was heavy clay and the Nicholson's had to bring in a bulldozer to help level the ground. The bulldozer came too close to the house and knocked in two walls of the basement. Maxine was alone at the house and had to call Ernie to come home from work. The Nicholson's made good on the damage to the house. The Nicholson's wre black people. They brought in a work crew of coloured men. They surrounded the house, propped it up, then dug all around the house and fixed the basement. They even sang songs while they worked.

Maxine and Ernie went to church at the Welland Avenue United Church. Alf and June were baptized at that church. Maxine started to go to West St. Andrews Church on Pelham Road. The minister was Reverend Taylor Monroe. Danny was born on August 31, 1952. Reverend Monroe baptized Danny and Phil. Phil was not born yet at this time. The mortgage was $3000 for the house. They paid $25 a month. Ernie's paycheck at that time at the Royal Bank was $25 a week. Ernie worked for extra money at the Legion tending bar.

The Royal Bank was remodeling and so Ernie took the opportunity of using material they were throwing out to finish the house. For example, He took linoleum which he used on the kitchen floor. And made a banister for the basement stairs. He made several lamps out of some of the wood he received. He worked at the bank or seven years. He also kept busy by taking correspondence courses. He quit working at the bank in 1952. He started working part time at bill Tempest Garage which was next door to the house as a mechanic. Bill Tempest sold out to Sterling Motors. Ernie bought into the company and continued to work there. Sterling Motors went bankrupt and Ernie was stuck with another mortgage. He had to look or another job again.

Ernie had bought a car (Willis) from Sterling Motors. He paid cash for it but some kind of swindling went on and Ernie ended up with a part ownership of house in the north end of St. Catharines. In the bankruptcy the larger companies took majority of the profits from Sterling Motors and Ernie only received a $100 from it. These were very tough times because they didn't know where food and coal were coming from. Ernie was active in all the functions at the Royal Canadian Legion Branch 24. The legion gave Maxine and Ernie a half ton of coal once to help them out.

In 1954, Ernie got a job at Direct Winters as a mechanic. Philip was born on November 21, 1954 in St.Catharines. Life was getting a little easier now as steady money now coming in and Maxine's mother and father and her brother Bill also helped them out too.

Now they started going on holidays. Dad always told me to start saving for two week holiday every year. Each week he told me to put a little away or holidays. So I have taken his advice and always made sure we went on holidays every year. Holidays were always special and we always had a good time. The first holiday they stayed at a cottage at Eire Beach for $15 a week. The cottage was owned by a friend of Ernie's sister Dora Perrin. They went back there every year for three years. Then they rented a cottage five units down from the one they had rented. The first time we were there we had to clean the cottage because it was a mess. That night when I was put to bed I remember looking up and seeing the stars through the roof. That night it rained and Mom and Dad put pots and pans all over the place to catch the drips from the leaking roof. Phil and I slept with a a big pot between us. During holidays we always had lots of company and always had a good time. They rented that cottage for a few years after.

Alf married Anne Blong on May 19, 1962 in Thorald, Ontario. Shari was born November 18, 1962. In August 1962 we went up to Billy Bear Lake at Huntsville wit Mary and Jake Peters. The next year we went to Heally Falls with Bea and Jim Erskine. We stayed in a cottage and they stayed in a camper that was in the back of a pick up truck. In 1964 we were invite to go for holidays with Alf and Anne at Tweed. Shari was just a baby and was a handful. Phil Stepped on a nail and had to be carried around. There was not a flat piece of land around and it was all rock. Missy our dog was just a pup, Missy ran down the rocky hill towards the lake and couldn't stop, She went right into the water head first. After that she was never afraid of the water,

The next year we went to Dunneville. Mom and us stayed with with Jane Dominey. Bill And Dad came to visit on the weekends.

In 1965 we started going up to Chubb Lake just outside Huntsville with Gil and Jean Blundell

June marrie Victor Shaw on March 8, 1967. Alf and Ann had their second child Michelle born on December 21, 1967.

In 1968 Ernie's mother became sick and was hospitalized for a month and she passed away on September 25, 1968. In 1969 we went west to Saskatchewan. Maxine and Ernie had not been back out there since 1946. Aunt Maude came with us. Ernie bought four brand new retread tires which he hoped would make it out there and back. Well they didn't. At the end of our trip we had four brand new tires. At one point Dad tried to sell the car when we got to a major city he was so mad. We had a great time. The next year we tried to rent the cottage at Chubb Lake but they were all booked so we rented a cottage on Peninsula Lake which was just on the other side of Huntsville with Gil and Jean Blundell.

Ernest Alfred Franklin Carter 1941

Ernest Alfred Franklin Carter was born on July 25, 1941 in St. Catharines. Alfred married Anne Blong on May 19, 1962 in Thorold. Anne Louise Blong was born on January 19, 1942 in St. Catharines. They have two children, Sharon Lynn and Michelle Leigh.

Alfred took his apprenticeship in refrigeration at J and S Refrigeration. He worked at the Ontario Paper mill as a refrigeration engineer. Anne worked in the paper mill office. Alf left the paper mill after seventeen years and went into a partnership with a refrigeration and environmental control company. After five years he became the manager of a refrigeration wholesaler in St. Catharines which he really enjoyed for a number of years. He made it one of the most profitable branches in Ontario but unfortunately the bank foreclosed on the company. He then worked at a major hotel in Niagara Falls as Chief Engineer. He retired at age 59 working for the Lennox Company designing the refrigeration for wineries in the Niagara Peninsula. Anne retired from the Ontario Paper mill after twenty three years with a full pension when the mill was sold. Alf and Anne love to travel and have seen a good part of the world, mainly in the Caribbean where they enjoyed snorkelling. For the past few years they have been spending part of the winter months in Myrtle Beach. They are living in Port Colborne. Alf has always been a dare devil. He was fascinated with flying, both real aircraft and model airplanes. He also loved to scuba dive. He was certified and then later became a YMCA scuba instructor. He and his diving buddies belonged to the Underwater Club of St. Catharines (the Barrucudas) where they taught for 20 years. They dove the Great Lakes, the St. Lawrence River, the Bahamas, Florida, Cozumel and Cuba. While ice diving in Lake Erie at the mouth of the Welland Canal, Alf's regulator froze shut. After a few hair raising moments he dragged his student and himself to the surface. That was IT for ice diving. Unfortunately, a photographer from The Standard was on the scene to relate the events. Alf later built a two-man wet submarine. He also built hang glider using a frame of aircraft aluminum pipes and parachute nylon for the sail. Phil, Gilles, Alf and myself went to Short Hills Park to try it out. Gilles harnessed himself in first, ran down the hill but didn't fly. Phil tried it next. He ran down the hill, started to take off so leaped into the air but crashed to the ground damaging the frame. He was scraped up pretty bad. Alf and I both declined the adventure. Alf later tried flying the glider towed behind a boat but found it too dangerous so he sold the glider. Alf has always had a boat - a racing boat, a sailboat and a cruiser, as well as many motorcycles. He obtained his International Ultralight license while vacationing on the Caribbean island of St. Martin. He later purchased an Ultralight plane, and then built a two-seater Home-Built aircraft from scratch which he flew from the St. Catharines airport. He and his friends from the RAA build model aircraft with gasoline engines.
