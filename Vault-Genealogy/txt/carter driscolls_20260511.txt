The Story of the Carter Family

Dan Carter's story begins in mid-20th-century Canada, but the roots of his family stretch across oceans- - from the crowded streets of London's East End, to rural England, Ireland, early New York colonies, and the Canadian prairies.

Dan was born in 1952 in St. Catharines, Ontario, A son of Ernest Carter and Maxine Dominey. His parents' lives already represented two powerful migration stories: one from England to western Canada, and another from England and Ireland through Ontario and Saskatchewan.

The Prairie Generation: Ernest and Maxine

Dan's father, Ernest Carter, was born in 1915 in Saskatoon, Saskatchewan. Ernest's parents - George Carter and Edith Sissen - had emigrated from England only a few years earlier, part of a wave of families seeking opportunity in Canada's growing prairie provinces.

Dan's mother, Maxine Dominey, was born in 1923 in Semans, Saskatchewan. Her father, Daniel Dominey, had been born in Liverpool, England, while her mother, Agnes Copeman, came from Ontario farming stock. By the time Ernest and Maxine married in 1940, the family had already woven together English urban workers, rural Ontario settlers, and prairie pioneers.

They eventually settled in St. Catharines, Ontario, where both are buried today.

C The English Roots: London, Essex, and Norfolk

On the Carter side, the line traces strongly into London's East End.

Dan's grandfather George Carter was born in Poplar, London in 1884. His father, Richard Carter, came from Shoreditch - an area known for working-class trades during the Industrial Revolution. Richard died young at only 33, leaving George to grow up during a period of hardship and rapid urban change.

George's wife, Edith Katurah Sissen, came from rural eastern England - Essex and Suffolk — where her Sissen ancestors had lived for generations. Her father Julian Sissen died in 1914, just as World War I began, and the family scattered across England, Canada, and even the United States in the following decades.

Early History of The Driscoll Clan

The original Eidirsceol, from whom the family is descended was born in the early 10th century. According to one legend, Lugh Ith was the leader of an expedition of Celts who arrived in the Baltimore area seeking to escape Roman domination. He was given the name Hy Drisceoil or O hEidersceol which comes from the Irish Eidersceol meaning "go between" or "bearer of news".

During the early Middle Ages the O'Driscolls were Admirals who comanded the fleets of the Kings of Munster. They also controlled a huge territory encompassing all of Bantry, Carbery and Beara baronies; an area co-extensive with the diocese of Ross, Around the close of the 12th century, pressure from the O'Sullivans drove them eastward, and they settled in the vicinity of Baltimore. Further encroachment by the O'Donavans and the O'Mahonys reduced the septs holdings to a narrow strip of seacoast. around the Bay of Baltimore.

Here in the year 1460, the Chief of the Sept founded a Franciscan monestary. Although their patrimony was vastly diminished, the O'Driscolls remained one of the leading maritime families in the region, retaining a number of strong fortified castles, down to the destruction of the Gaelic order in the 17th century. Many of the name played an important role in the Munster wars during the reign of Elizabeth I. Staunch supporters of James II, several O'Driscolls were officers in his Irish army. As a result of the Jacobites' defeat at the Battle of Kinsale in 1690, th O'Driscolls' property was regranted to Lord Castlehaven. At this time, the name was also prominant in the roles of Irish Brigades in the service of France, Austria and Spain. One of the exiles, Col. Cornelius O'Driscoll, greatly distinguished himself at the Battle of Ondara in 1707. Although the O'Driscolls suffered extensive losses in the Cornwellian and Williamite confiscations and resettlements, today in Ireland, the majority of this sept reside in or not far from their ancestral lands.

Daniel Dominey 1895

Daniel Dominey was born on June 7,1895 in Poplar, London England. Daniel parents were William John Dominey and Mary Driscol. They had another child named William whom was born on December 6, 1898. in London, England.. Both parents died during a plague that swept the Liverpool area.

Mary died November 27,1902 and William John died May 27,1904. Dan and William were placed in Mrs. Louisa Birt's Orphanage at Stifford Home near Gray's Essex after their father died in 1904. Their Grandmother Jane Dominey was still living and she lived in the outskirts of London at Poplar Union Workhouse. Daniel was sent to Liverpool Sheltering House on September 13,1907.

Louisa Birt was in a program where they sent English children to Canada for Adoption. Dan was sent to Canada aboard the SS Grampion on February 20,1908. They

landed in New Brunswick and then was sent to one of her orphanage houses in Quebec. Dan stayed at the orphanage named Knowlton House which was in the town of Knowlton, Quebec. Dan was adopted by a Mr. and Mrs. Robert Tracy. The Tracy family had a farm in Shawville, Quebec. Dan was adopted like many of the orphans sent over as cheap child labour to work the farm. The Tracy's already had a family and Dan was lucky because they treated him kindly. They sent him to school but he said he knew more than the teachers did. The Tracy's taught him to read and write. The Tracy's paid Dan a salary of $36.00 a year for his wages plus free room and board. They also had to buy Dan's clothes not less than $12.00 yearly till sixteen.

While Dan was in Canada he sent letters to a Mrs. C.M.J. Mc Dowell at Fort Coulage, Quebec and she in turn mailed letters to the orphanage of Dan's well being. The orphanage had liaison officers who checked on adopters to make sure that they were mistreated. Dan kept in touch with his brother by sending him mail. William was. sent to Liverpool Sheltering Homes in October 5, 1909. On February 18,1910 William was sent to Canada and he sailed on the S.S. Corsican. William was adopted by a family who treated him badly. He kept on running away. One time he came for a visit and stayed a the Tracy farm. The Tracy's let him stay and treated him as one of them. Mrs. Tracy died of a plague that was supposed to have been brought back from returning soldiers. Dan didn't contact the plague and he helped drive the doctor around to see the sick. A lot of the times they would arrive at a household to find them all dead. Mr. Tracy remarried again but his new wife didn't want Dan and William there.

The next four years I do not know what happened to William. On his Attestation Papers for the army He was a farmer and his address was Manitock Station, Ontario which is 10 miles outside Ottawa.

Dan left the Tracy farm and got a job at Strong Lumber Company No. 1 Camp Cache Bay just outside Temagami, in a logging camp. They would fall a tree and Dan with a team his own horses would drag the tree down to the water. He worked his way across Ontario. He would put his team of horses on the train and go to the next camp. He told me he was so proud of the performance of his horses. One horse was named Bell and I cannot remember the name of the other. In March 16,1914, Dan worked for a farmer at Yarm Quebec. I looked for Yarm but couldn't find it later on someone came across it and ound out was a town of yesterday and the Yarm Cemetery is still there. It is just outside of Shawville Quebec.


William enlisted in the army January 29, 1917. He was serving with 240th Overseas BN C.E.F. at Renfrew. William was sent to England and arrived on June 14, 1917 where he was placed into the 7th Reserve Battalion at Seaford. Then he was transfered to the 21st Battalion (Eastern Ontario) on September 13, 1917. The 21st Battalion was placed into the 2nd Canadian Division. On September 1st the 2nd Division was given the orders to take Passchendale Ridge. William was killed in action on November 3rd or 4th 1917 just outside Passchendale and is buried at Tyne-Cott Cemetery Passchendale, Belgium.

Daniel married Bernice Hodgins on August 7, 1918. One day he came home from the field and found her with another man. I do not know whether he did get a divorce or not. The Government advertised free land out west. So he went out to Raymore, Saskatchewan. He received a quarter section of land outside of Semans.

Dan could not make a go of the farming so he sold the property. He then hired himself and his team of horses to other farmers in the area. Some of the farms he worked on were George and Tom Lewis, Burt and Ross James and G.E. Orser.

Dan lived in the town of Semans. Dan was looking for a woman to clean his house and that was when he met Agnes Margaret Copeman. Agnes and Dan were married in November 15, 1926. Together they had two children Maxine and William. Maxine was born April 7, 1923 and William was born June 30, 1931.

Agnes sold corsets and used to drive John Copeman's car. John Copeman used to always have a new Ford every year. During the winter Dan would haul wood at Wynyard and to the Indian Reservation jist outside Raymore. He got to know the Carter Family. The Carter homestead was on the edge of the reservation ten miles out of town of Raymore Agnes parents, John and Jenny Copeman wanted them to move to Ontario because John was sick. In 1934 Agnes and Dan and family moved to Tillsonburg to be with her parents. John Copeman died before they arrived. The farm was part of a ownership between Jenny and her two brothers, Charles and Robert. Nellie also lived at the farm. Dan worked at the farm for three months but couldn't get along with Robert and Charles. So he moved to Courtland where he worked on the Twist farm. Then he moved to the Helsdon farm which was out-side Courtland towards Tillsonburg. Then Dan moved back to Courtland to work on the Boughner farm. Maxine met Ernest Carter

and they were married in August 18,1940 at the Boughner farm. Ernie Joined the Army and went to Camp Petawawa at Pembroke Ontario. Maxine went to Delhi to stay with Jenny Copeman. Maxine got her own apartment and Agnes and Dan moved from Courtland to Delhi to be with her, the year was 1940. Dan worked at odd jobs in Delhi. One day he received a letter from an old friend from Shawville, Quebec who owned a Creamery in Tillsonburg about a job in St. Catharines at the General Motors plant. So he took a bus to St. Catharines and went to the General Motors personnel office. He was dressed in a suit and had a doctors bag with him. When he arrived there, there was a line up and when they saw him coming towards them they all parted and let him walk right into the office, thinking he was a doctor. Dan was hired on the spot. Dan rented an apartment on Ontario Street next to the plant. He then sent for Agnes to join him. When Agnes and Bill arrived in St. Catharines.

Information: the Little Immigrants

During my research, I was looking for information on why Dan and William came to Canada and I found a book on The Little Immigrants by Kenneth Bagnell. In it explains how it came about that they were sent to Canada.

In 1866, there was an epidemic of cholera that sweep through London and most of the people died before reaching the age of twenty. There were lots of children on the streets abandoned and with no parents.

In 1868, Louisa Birt and her two sisters Rachel and Anna started an orphanage. In 1869,

They had opened up four new homes in London. The amount of young people on the streets and in orphanages started to become a problem. The New Brunswick government had heard of England's problem and asked if they could take the children and place them for adoption to area farmers to help with there farm work. The government decided to send the boys and girls of orphanages between the ages of five and twelve years to Canada. Louisa Birt traveled to Canada and started up orphanage here in Canada to receive and be set up for distribution of the children. In 1873, Louisa started an orphanage in Liverpool. Cholera epidemic had swept through the city and industry was down. A lot of children were abandoned by their parents or their parents were dead. In 1873 Louisa started an orphanage in Knowlton, Quebec which is seventy miles from Montreal.

When the children landed in Canada, they had to sign a contract with the adopted parents. They had to stay with the adopted parents till they were eighteen for a boy and twenty-one for a girl. But legally stay till they were sixteen years old. On the farm they were expected on to work as hard as their adopted parents children were expected. Most cases they didn't. The children were sent all across Canada.

Dan and William Dominey were placed in Louisa Birt's Home in Liverpool and in Feb. 20,1908 they were sent to Canada.

Abigail (Jackson) Becker Abigail (Jackson) Becker was a second cousin of Eleanor Jane Jackson Pick, our grandmother, a large woman, the size of Lettie Harriet Pick Learn. She was the wife of a trapper at Long Point on Lake Erie. One stormy night in 1854 the three- masted schooner, Conductor, grounded off shore. The captain, a man by the name of Haskett, his crew of six and the ships cooked seemed doomed to be washed overboard. They had tried to take shelter from the storm and been swept on a sand bar in the night..

Abigail saw them in the morning while getting a pail of water out of the lake. Her husband was away and there was no one within reach to help. She got her tea kettle, some tea and matches. She started a fire on shore and heaped it with wood so the men would know that help was at hand.

The question was how could she help as no boat was available and the water was so rough she would not be able to control it, if she had one. Abigail dashed into the water and beckoned to the men to make an effort to reach her. It was their only chance, the captain was the first to swim towards her.. He was caught in the undertow and was about to be swept away when Abigail went out and brought him into shore. After leaving him by the fire, she waded out and saved them all one by one.. She also saved her crippled son who had tried to help..

The cook was saved the next day by the crew who made a raft after the storm had abated; he had lashed himself to the mast because he was unable to swim. She was credited with saving the eight men; the water had been bitter cold and the resue had taken great courage and endurance. Tyne Cot Cemetery, Passchendaele, West-Vlaanderen

Details

Tyne Cot Cemetery is located 9 kilometres north east of Ieper town centre, on the Tynecotstraat, a road leading from the Zonnebeekseweg (N332).

'Tyne Cot' or 'Tyne Cottage' was the name given by the Northumberland Fusiliers to a barn which stood near the level crossing on the Passchendaele-Broodseinde road. The barn, which had become the centre of five or six German blockhouses, or pill-boxes, was captured by the 2nd Australian Division on 4 October 1917, in the advance on Passchendaele. One of these pill-boxes was unusually large and was used as an advanced dressing station after its capture. From 6 October to the end of March 1918. 343 graves were made, on two sides of it, by the 50th (Northumbrian) and 33rd Divisions, and by two Canadian units. The cemetery was in German hands again from 13 April to 28 September. when it was finally recaptured, with Passchendaele, by the Belgian Army. TYNE COT CEMETERY was greatly enlarged after the Armistice when remains were brought in from the battlefields of Passchendaele and Langemarck, and from a few small burial grounds. It is now the largest Commonwealth war cemetery in the world in terms of burials. At the suggestion of King George V, who visited the cemetery in 1922, the Cross of Sacrifice was placed on the original large pill-box. There are three other pill-boxes in the cemetery. There are now 11,953 Commonwealth servicemen of the First World War buried or commemorated in Tyne Cot Cemetery. 8,366 of the burials are unidentified but there are special memorials to more than 80 casualties known or believed to be buried among them. Other special memorials commemorate 20 casualties whose graves were destroyed by shell fire. The cemetery was designed by Sir Herbert Baker. The TYNE COT MEMORIAL forms the north-eastern boundary of Tyne Cot Cemetery and commemorates nearly 35,000 servicemen from the United Kingdom and New Zealan who died in the Ypres Salient after 16 August 1917 and whose graves are not known. The memorial stands close to the farthest point in Belgium reached by Commonwealth forces in the First World War until the final advance to victory. The memorial was designed by Sir Herbert Baker with sculpture by F
