The Berg family's migration path is nearly identical to the Rempels and Sawatzkys, as they were all part of the same Colony movement. Their journey spanned four countries over 400 years.

1. The Immediate Origin: Bergthal, Russia (Ukraine)

Just before arriving in Manitoba, your specific Berg ancestors lived in the Bergthal Colony in South Russia (located in the government of Yekaterinoslav, now the Donetsk/Zaporizhzhia region of

Ukraine).

The Departure: In the 1870s, the Russian government began removing the special privileges (like military exemption) given to Mennonites.

The Total Migration: Unlike other groups who sent only a few families, the entire Bergthal Colony-every single person decided to sell their land and move to Canada.

The Ship: Most Bergs arrived in Canada between 1874 and 1876. They traveled on steamships like the SS Quebec and the SS Peruvian, landing in Quebec City before traveling by rail and riverboat to Manitoba.

2. The Middle Origin: West Prussia (Danzig)

Before moving to Russia in 1836, the Bergs lived in West Prussia (now northern Poland), specifically in the Marienwerder and Danzig (Gdańsk) districts.

Village Roots: The name Berg is very old in Mennonite records, frequently appearing in Prussian villages like Heubuden and Ladekopp.

The Move to Russia: They left Prussia because of new taxes on Mennonite land and the growing pressure to join the Prussian military. They moved to Russia because the Czar promised them free land and the right to live in closed, German-speaking communities.

3. The Deepest Roots: The Netherlands

While the family spent centuries in Prussia and Russia, the "Berg" name (meaning "Mountain" or "Hill" in German/Dutch) traces back to the Low Countries (the Netherlands and Belgium).

• The 1500s: During the Reformation, the Bergs were early followers of Menno Simons. To escape religious persecution by the Catholic Spanish rulers of the Netherlands, they fled east along the Baltic Sea to the Vistula Delta in Prussia.

1730 Jacob Berg Prussia

Tracing Jacob Berg (born c. 1730) takes us to the very beginning of the formal Mennonite records in West Prussia. Because the Prussian government conducted a famous census in 1776, we have a "snapshot" of his life during that era.

1. Where He Lived: The Vistula Delta

Jacob Berg lived in the Großes Werder (the Great Delta) region of West Prussia. This area was then part of the Kingdom of Prussia (now northern Poland, near the city of Gdańsk).

Village: He is most strongly associated with the village of Tiegenhagen or the nearby Heubuden district.

The Landscape: He lived on "polder" land-low-lying marshes that the Mennonites had drained using windmills and canals.

Status: In the 1776 Census, he is listed as a "Landwirt" (farmer). He did not own his land outright (as the King restricted Mennonite land ownership), but he held a long-term lease that was passed down through the family.

2. Marriage and Family

While the exact maiden name of his wife is often missing from early Prussian church books (which sometimes only recorded the husband's name), we know he was married by 1754.

Wife: Records suggest her name was Maria or Barbara (born c. 1735).

The 1776 Census Record: Jacob is listed in the Tiegenhagen district with the following family composition:

• 1 Male (Jacob himself) • 1 Female (His wife) • 3 Sons • 2 Daughters

3. His Children

Of his children, the most important for your lineage is the son who moved the family to Russia.

2. Johann Berg (born c. 1760): Remained in Prussia for a time; some of his descendants stayed in Germany/Poland until the 1940s.

3. Abraham Berg (born c. 1765): Also recorded in early Prussian land records.

4. Why the 1730 Jacob is the "Anchor"

Jacob (1730) lived through a massive political shift. In 1772, the area moved from Polish rule to Prussian rule. The new Prussian King, Frederick the Great, valued the Bergs for their farming but feared their pacifism (they refused to join the army).

This pressure is exactly why his son, Jacob (1755), decided to leave the family home in Tiegenhagen and move to Russia. If the 1730 Jacob hadn't been a successful farmer, his son wouldn't have had the resources to make that massive journey.

At this time, Jacob Berg was established in the village of Pletzendorf (near Tiegenhagen). The Fire Tax was an early form of insurance; families paid into a communal fund so that if a thatched roof caught fire, the community could rebuild it.
