The Jackson's

The Jackson family tree has been the most documented. Karen Boland from Hamilton shared a good deal of information with me. With further research I have found additional information. It was believed that the Jackson's came to United States around middle of 1700's but they were already here in North America. Karen Boland has it in her records that the Jackson's came to United States and lived in Philadelphia. I looked long hours to make a connection of them to that area. I found a file on the internet on the historical background of William Jackson a son of Pieter Jackson form the Historical Atlas of the county of Middlesex. It is an article related by Nathaniel, William's son and is written in the third person. It stated that:

In 1750 Henry Jackson who was a sea captain settled in Pennsylvania where his son Pieter accumulated a large property. After the War of Independence, Pieter abandoned his property to confiscation rather than forswear allegiance to the King he had served and to the flag under which he had fought during the war. Hendrick and Pieter left their possessions and emigrated to Canada where they received a grant of land in the Ottawa district.

The Jackson's may have settled in Pennsylvania first but (Pieter Jackson's brother) James Jackson's Land Grant Petition states that they were from Coxsackie, New York, just south of Albany. Karen Boland had in her research that Pieter Jackson was a constable in the police force in Philadelphia before they came to Canada. Something 1 found interesting was a family tree file generated by Mary Ann Kircus. She is a descendant of the Jackson's. The surname Jackman was believed to be the correct spelling and when they came to United States changed it to Jackson.

The first known descendants of the Jackson was Hendrick Jackson and Sarah Clute. I haven't be able to find a lead for a Jackson before Hendrick. I presume that he might have came over from Germany, England or Holland. Karen Boland's and Anne Kircus records state that the Jacksons were from Germany. Sarah Clute on the other hand has family tree records posted on the Latter Day Saint's site. Sarah's Grandparents on her mother's side were Nicholas VanVranken and Geertray Quackenbos. On her father side was Frederick Cloete and Francine DuMond. These two family units can be trace farther back but I have not documented any farther on this line. The spelling of the last name Knoet, Cleote, Kloet and Clute is recorded in different documents found by different family members. That was a common occurrence in researching family lines which I have found and have benefited by changing the spelling of first and last names for results. Albany is one of the oldest settlements in America. The Dutch arrived the area in 1610. They built a fort in 1614, which was replaced by a stockade on 1623. This was the first settle and beginning of Albany. The first 40 years of church records of Albany are not

available and transcripts just only began in 1683, which is still remarkable for keeping records in that time period.

Jackson History as per William's son Nathaniel

" 'In the Historical Atlas of the County of Middlesex, Ontario published in 1878, William Jackson's son Nathaniel tells the following story about the family: (Note: originally written in third person.)

==> Of the pioneers of Ontario, none have shewn (sic) more patience, or contributed more to the improvement of the Province than the United Empire Loyalists and their descendants. In 1750 my great great grandfather who was a sea captain, settled in Pennsylvania where his son Peter (my great grandfather) accumulated a large property. After the war of Independence this though old loyalist abandoned his property to confiscation rather than forswear allegiance to the King he had served, and to the flag under which he had fought during the war. His son James, who was my grandfather, was also engaged during the war as a sturdy defender of the throne.

Father and son however, unhesitatingly left their possessions and emigrated to Canada, where they received grants of land in the Ottawa District. William, my father, settled on a lot north of Kingston, but finding the soil rocky and unproductive, he sold out and after a short time settled in what was then considered the "far west," but is now fitly termed the "Garden of Canada."

This was in 1851 and I was then only 15 years old but was possessed of a vigorous frame and a robust constitution. The family settled on Lot 21 Centre Road, West Williams, and in order to reach the lot we were obliged to chop a road two miles through the woods. This was accomplished to a great extent by me as I was the only son old enough to work, and father (who died in 1872) was old and feeble and unable to render much aid in the heavy work of making roads or clearing the farm.

At that time, deer, wolves and bears were plentiful in West Williams and venison being easily procured was a common article of food with the early settlers. <===

There are errors in Nathaniel Jackson's account, probably because he was relating oral history. THE HEROINE OF LONG POINT In November 1854 the schooner "Conductor" was wrecked off this shore during one of Lake Erie's many violent storms. Jeremiah Becker, who resided nearby, was away on the mainland but his courageous wife, Abigail, risked her life by repeatedly entering the water while assisting the exhausted seamen to reach land. The eight sailors were housed and fed in her cabin until they recovered from their ordeal. In recognition of her heroism she received a letter of commendation from Queen Victoria, several financial awards, and a gold medal from the Life Saving Benevolent Association of New York.

Conductor: Schooner driven ashore on 11/24/1854 on Long Point, Ontario and broken apart in a gale. This wreck led to what is perhaps the most legendary rescue on the lakes. As the Conductor began to break up and sink in the surf 1/2 mile offshore, a poor woman living nearby named Abigail "Mother "Becker saw the nine man crew in distress and climbing the masts to avoid the icy water. She was a big woman and couldn't afford to buy the special large shoes she required and therefore went barefoot. Since her husband was away and the nearest neighbor lived 14 miles distant, Mother Becker started a fire on the beach to coax the survivors to swim to shore, but not one tried. At this she waded and swam out to the sinking vessel through the surf and dragged the crewmen, starting with the captain, one by one back to the beach. In all she made nine trips to the boat and saved the lives of the entire crew! The story of this deed was widely published in the papers of the time, and was recorded in a poem by Whittier. As a reward for her efforts the Becker family received money, shoes, and a 100 acre farm. For decades following this event steamers sounded their whistles as a tribute as they passed the Becker's house.

In 1869 Abigail married Henry Rohrer. They settled on Abigail's 50 acre farm. Of this union there were 3 daughters making a total family of 17. Later 2 more children were added by adoption. (from Karen M. Boland, Hamilton, Ont. Canada)

As the story of Abigail's courageous rescue became known among the captains and crews of the ships that plied the Creat Lakes, they spread the tale far and wide. She received a number of honours, many of them through the efforts of E.P. Dorr, a local captain. A banquet was given in her honour in Buffalo, at which she was given a purse of $500, most of it contributed by sailors. A letter signed by Queen Victoria herself acknowledged her feat and enclosed a gift of £50. In 1860 the Prince of Wales presented a further gift while he was duck hunting on Long Point.

And the governor- general sent a belated congratulatory letter in the 1890s. The Benevolent Life Saving Association of New York wished to recognize the heroine. President Joseph Walker wrote Captain Dorr: "The giving of our medal is confined to the saving of American life. If thee wilt find that there was an American life saved from on board Schooner Conductor, Abigail shall have our best medal.' "

On enquiry, Captain Dorr learned that Jerome, the mate, and a crew member were, in fact, American seamen. The medal was struck and sent to the captain with instructions to obtain a receipt for it. He sent it to the col-lector of customs in Port Rowan, who was nonplussed for a moment when, on asking that Abigail give him a receipt for the medal, discovered she could not write. But he solved the problem by suggesting she accompany him to a photographer's where a daguerrotype was taken of Abigail proudly display-ing her new bible and the medal.

The people of Long Point Bay still honor Abigail Becker. In commemoration of her amazing feat more than 120 years ago her portrait hangs in the Abigail Becker Ward of Simcoe Town Hospital.

Looking for a fight Newport: a group of intruders burst through Copeman's door, threatening to show "What an Irish mob can do "The ugly events that followed left one man dead and another facing a charge of murder.

Heather Ibbotson Saturday July 14, 2007- 9:00

Weekend - William Anderson and his tough guy pals were looking for a fight on the night of May 18, 1863 and didn't care if women and children got caught in the crossfire. The four twenty somethings had plenty of liquid courage coursing through their veins when they hatched an ill-considered plan to bust into the Brantford Township home of Benjamin Copeman and set the older man straight on a few things.

The ugly events that followed left one man dead and another facing a charge of murder. Earlier that day, Ben Copeman, whom was about 48 had quarreled with the Andersons at a logging bee held at the nearby Anderson family homestead.

The Copeman and Anderson families were connected by marriage; Ben's daughter, Elizabeth, was married to John Anderson.

Ben took issue with what he viewed as his son-in-law's shoddy treatment of Elizabeth, specifically John's alleged failure to buy shoes for her.

Walking home from the bee, Ben had bent the ear of his son-in-law's brother, George, with bitter complaints about John. The words still stung later that night when George relayed them to his brother.

Brothers John, George and William Anderson, along with a friend, Charles Roleson, sat at the home of neighbour William Legacy and rehashed the day while they shared some more drinks.

Tempers soon flared and any sparks of common sense were doused by the alcohol. John suggested they head over to Ben's house and confront the older man.

intruders

The Copeman house was divided into two parts. Ben and his wife, Hannah, lived in one section, while a separate part housed Ben's son, John; his wife, Mary; their infant son; and Mary's mother, Mary Toland.

It was between 9 p.m. and 10 p.m. when the group of intruders burst through Copeman's door, threatening to show "what an Irish mob can do." Angry words flew, women screamed, heavy objects flew threw the air and property was smashed.

When it was over, William Anderson lay mortally wounded. An ugly gash gaped red and wet at his neck.


Roleson and John Anderson carried William back to Legacy's house. Roleson later said William had mumbled for a doctor and declared he was bleeding to death.

George said the fateful blow came from an axe wielded by John Wesley Copeman, Ben's son. William lingered at death's door. Dr. Robert Hill Dee was called the next morning and he arrived to find William in bed with dreadful wounds to his lower jaw and neck.

He had a cut that began at the back of the jaw and extended along the jaw line to the chin. The jaw was broken and the bones could be wiggled by hand. The gash had sliced into William's windpipe and as the dying man laboured to breathe, air escaped through the wound in his neck. Death claimed him that day.

The autopsy concluded the cause of death was loss of blood and shock as a result of a neck wound inflicted by a sharp object. An inquest was held.

Jurors gazed on the corpse and heard testimony about the fracas before concluding that "the deceased came to his death by the blow of some sharp instrument, in the hands of John Copeman." Copeman, 23, was arrested and held in the Brant County Jail on Market Street to await trial on a charge of murder.

The trial took place at the Brant County fall assizes held in late October 1863 in the main courtroom of the Brant County Courthouse on Wellington Street. It was held under the same 1852 fir plank ceiling that today's spectators gaze at more than 140 years later.

Brantford Township resident William Legacy told court he was present when Ben Copeman argued with George Anderson on the way home from the bee and when the Andersons and Roleson were in his house, drinking, and planning the ambush.

Legacy said he followed the men to within 16 feet of Copeman's house. He saw John Copeman leave the house, climb on a shed and return to the house on two separate occasions but he saw no weapon in his hand. He also heard a shout that Billy was dead and saw the man's limp form being carried from the scene.

Legacy's testimony suffered when he admitted under cross-examination that he had testified at the inquest to being 20 yards from the residence, not 16 feet. When asked why he had changed his testimony, he replied, "Because I think so now."

chance

George and John Anderson gave versions of events that sounded as though the intruders had simply walked to the Copeman house to offer Ben a chance to speak his mind to his son-in-law. John said that Ben was friendly, saying "good evening" when they walked in, until he was asked if he had a problem with John.

They said that Ben picked up a chair and dropped it before grabbing a butcher knife. He jabbed at Roleson and drove the men out. George said he saw his brother get hit by something, but was not sure what. Under cross-examination, George said he had not used violence or threats before Ben picked up

the chair and that no damage was done to other parts of the Copeman house.

John and Charles Roleson admitted they pulled a wooden rail off a nearby fence and heaved it through the door.

Roleson said he saw John Copeman hit William with an axe and saw Ben throw a brick. Roleson admitted he had been drinking steadily all day, at the logging bee and at Legacy's house, but he did not consider himself drunk.

Witnesses for the defence provided more detail of the uproar that engulfed the Copeman home on May 18.


Mary Toland said she was going to bed when she heard a noise, stepped to the door separating the living areas of the house, and saw the Andersons and Roleson confronting Ben. She said George Anderson was eye to eye with Ben and had threatened to show him what an Irish

mob can do. The other men were ranged behind George in a show of force. George then slapped Ben across the face. Ben ordered the men out of his house and called to Hannah and Mary. "Women, come here. I call you to witness I have ordered these men out of my house three times," Ben said.

Mary told the court that Ben brandished a knife and demanded the intruders leave. The men went out, ran around the house and tossed something through a window. Shattered glass fell all over the baby and the cradle.

Mary ran to the window and begged the Andersons: "Boys, don't hurt us women, we have done you no harm."

The reply was vicious. "They swore they would have our heart's blood," she said. Her vivid words fell on a hushed courtroom; spectators and the all-male jury were aghast. Mary continued, saying that her daughter plucked the baby from his cradle and began screaming, "My baby is dead! My baby is dead!"

The baby was, in fact, not dead but the situation was so frightening and frantic that the young mother had become hysterical.

Mary Toland said she then heard a pounding at the door to their section of the house.

Moments later, she heard a grunt and then someone yelled that Billy was dead.

Mary bolted from the house through the back door and ran to Legacy's. She said she had been afraid for her life.

Hannah Copeman told much the same story. She had been with her husband and son when the intruders arrived between 9 p.m. and 10 p.m., threatening and committing violence. She said that the house was dark after her husband cleared the men out. The lamplight had been knocked out during the ruckus.

Thinking the worst over, Hannah went to another room to get matches when something smashed against the door, followed by shattering glass in another room.

She heard her daughter-in-law screaming about her baby and heard men outside cursing and threatening murder.

A moment later, a hard pounding on the door sent Hannah scurrying to brace herself against its frame. She was startled to hear exclamations from outside that Billy was dead and she momentarily relaxed her pressure on the door.

John and George Anderson burst back inside. George was armed with a hoe. Hannah was alone. It seemed everyone else had fled.

The Andersons searched the house to no avail. When they returned to Hannah, they had a gun, she said. Then they left.

fire

Ben Copeman was the next witness. He said he had been sitting with his wife and son by the fire in their home when their peace was interrupted.

He said that George Anderson slapped his son and then him, asking what did he have to say about the Irish now? Roleson grabbed Ben's hair and pulled him to the floor.

Ben ordered the men out but they did not leave so he picked up a chair and ordered them out again. He called the women to witness his demand that the trespassers leave. Finally able to clear the men out,


Ben closed and fastened the door.

Suddenly, a wooden rail flew through the window at the top of the door, hit him and knocked him to the floor.

Ben said the men still tried to get in the house, all the while cursing and yelling threats. Jurors also heard from two police constables who testified that the Copeman residence had been turned upside down, was littered with broken furniture and possessions and was obviously the scene of terrible violence.

Two other witnesses, George Barton and John Whiting, testified to the good character of young John Copeman.

The The jury heard final arguments by Crown attorney Wood and defence counsel O'Reilly. judge then reviewed the evidence for the jury and explained the law as it related to murder, manslaughter and justifiable homicide. The jury filed into the jury room to deliberate. After two hours of discussion, a verdict was reached. "Not Guilty."

A ripple of approval spread through the spectator's gallery.

John Copeman left the court a free man.

branches

The Copeman and Anderson families moved a great deal, mostly throughout southwestern Ontario, but branches also spread to the U.S. and Saskatchewan.

In the late 1870s and early 1880s, John Copeman's family, John Anderson's family and the elder Copemans all lived in southern Oxford County.

John Copeman raised a family with his wife Mary until her death in 1888 in Oxford County. He remarried a year later and raised a second family with Roxolena Chambers, of Norfolk County. It is not known if the Anderson and Copeman families were ever able to reconcile after this- perhaps Brant County's first home invasion with fatal consequences. -

THE SETTLERS OF THE BEEKMAN PATENT Chapter 55

The Coopman/Copeman Family

The Coopmans came to Beekman from New Jersey in 1761 and settled in lot 17 near the Rombout line. [We have not found any genealogies on this familyJ.

The emigrant was JOHANNES 1 COOPMAN who came to this country on the ship Allen which sailed from Rotterdam and landed 15 Sept. 1729 in Philadelphia, PA. There were a number of Palatines on board. Johannes settled in East New Jersey and was a customer at the Janeway store from Dec. 1736 through June 1747. [Gen. Mag. of NJ 33:1J. He m. Eve, possibly Reasoner, and if so, a sister to Jacob Reasoner who was also in the same area of New Jersey and came to Beekman.

Johannes coopman came to Beekman by June 1761 when he was first taxed and was assessed in Beekman Precinct in June 1767 at £2, and in June 1770 at £1. "Man on John Coopman's farm" was assessed at £2 in 1775 and 1778. He settled on a farm that had been leased by Jacobus Van Steinburgh, a wheelright. Coopman was also a wheelright and possibly took over the business. This farm was in the northern section of lot 17 and adjoined the Rombout Patent on the west. The old house on this property is still (1995) standing. The following entries in the rent book tell a little about how Coopman obtained this farm. "4 July 1764, (steinburgh) settled his back rents & bond as viz; his bond of 30 May 1761 for £18, with interest to this day is £3/17/9, is £21/17/9 and with 3 years rent at 19 bushels for 2 years at 7sh and one year 4/8 is the whole £17/13/8 so the bond and rent together is £39/11/5 and he being very likely hath resigned his farm to Mr. Coopman who together with his son John Copeman Jr. gave bond for £39/11/5 which clears this farm 1 May 1764." The Coopmans paid rents of 19 bushels in 1766 and 38 in 1769.

On 3 Oct. 1763 John Coopman gave a bond to Peter Montfort for £200 which probably helped finance the farm. He paid £6/6 towards the bond on 25 Dec. 1764 and on 9 March 1767 Montfort sold the bond to Isaac Ter Bos.

By order of the Honorable Cornelius Humphrey, Esq. one of the judges of the Inferior Court of Common Pleas for the County of Dutchess: NOTICE is hereby given to all the creditors of Jacob Coapman, an insolvant debtor of the said county, to appear (if so they or any of them think proper) before the said Judge at the house of Stephen Hendrickson, in Poughkeepsie, on Monday the eighteenth day of September instant, at two o'clock in the afternoon of the said day to shew cause if any they have, why as assignment of the said insolvent's estate should not be made for the use of all his creditors, and why he should not be discharged according to the term of the act, entitled "AN ACT for the relief of insolvent debtors", passed the 13th day of April, 1786.
