Our Legacy Begins


Roots: The Beginning McFadden's From Ireland

Looking into the past to find about our heritage is forever ongoing. Researching back into history, finding out what it was like to begin a new life in a new country. The unknown they ventured into, the hardships, they conquered to make a better life for their families. For me to sit here in the comfort of my own home, in which I have every conceivable comfort. Here I am looking into the past never thinking that our relatives came to Canada when there were few or no roads to travel on. Waterways were still being used for main transportation routes. Canada was an unsettled wilderness. The main towns were just beginning. Looking into the history of England and Canada in the late 1700 and early 1800's. I discovered and was amazed at how primitive and barren Canada was.

For example: The town of Prescott, a major port for arriving new settlers consisted of a church, a few houses and a trading post. The war of 1812 was over and the need to settle Upper Canada was being pushed. England had already started to disband their military regiments in England. There was high unemployment and the government decided to make her soldiers, settlers in the new world. These soldiers chose their land and without expense: Each man received according to their rank from 100 acres to 500 acres. They were supplied with all the necessary implements of husbandry, tools for building purposes and cooking utensils. In addition one years provisions for each man, women and child. To keep the land they had to prove cultivation and make improvements of such property. Later they could apply for Crown Patent. This package was also offered to civilians in 1816. A lot of early settlers frustrated, started to leave these properties. The work was too hard, the free provisions had run out and they just abandoned the land. The vacant land then was allotted to new settlers.

I was wondering what it was like to cross the ocean in those times and came across a journal about a man and his wife crossing from @ Ireland to Canada in 1817. It took an average of 40 days to cross the Atlantic Ocean. The ships were sailing schooners and were at the mercy of the wind and weather. Some settlers were sea sick the whole voyage over. Some of them never made the journey and were buried at sea. The longer they were at sea the living conditions deteriorated more. They ran out of fresh water or it went bad. One settler on that voyage can not believe the different ways to eat cabbage. To land at Quebec City was such a reverence but later the work to clear land, to farm, the mosquitoes, black flies and the elements made it unbearable. It was not an easy life they chose but they survived.

Our first descendants boarded a ship in Ireland. Thirteen in all and traveled across the Atlantic and landed at Quebec City safely. The courage they had and the hardships to follow must of been tremendous.

With the enlargement of the canals, trade rapidly increased but even after the improvement, transporting a cargo from Montreal to Kingston was a major undertaking. The bateaux and Durham boats, a later type of boat, illustrated in Fig. 3 were usually taken upstream in "brigades" of five or more so that, when the occasion arose, the crews would be able to assist each other. The brigades were sometimes assembled at Lachine, their cargoes having been brought from Montreal by road and at other times towed and rowed through the Lachine channel. At Cascades Point about three quarters of the cargo of a Durham boat was unloaded and carted to the head of the Cedars rapids. The boat was then locked through the Cascades and Split Rock canals and dragged up the Cedars rapids where the cargo was reloaded. The boat then passed through the Coteau lock and into Lake St. Francis. At the Long Sault rapids above Cornwall the process was repeated after which the boat could be sailed to Kingston. After 1818 a steam towboat was used in this section but unfortunately no details of this vessel can be found. On the average it took 12 days to transport the 8-ton cargo of a Durham boat from Montreal to Kingston.

The Beginning

Stringer Mcfadden was born in Armagh County Ireland on Sept. 5,1774. He married Sarah (Saragh) Amelia in 1797. Karen Boland has Sarah's last name recorded in her family tree as Aurelia. We have it in our records as Amelia. Sarah was the daughter of an Unknown male Amelia, born in Scotland and Jane(Coulter) Amelia, her mother, who was from Scotland. The Amelia's moved from Scotland to Armagh, Ireland when she was very small. I have searched for the last name Amelia and Aurelia and have come up with no results. The name Amelia seems to be of Spainish origin. It was said in our first records that I acquired stated that they were of English descent.

The Mcfaddens owned and operated linen mills in Ireland. In the late 1700's there was an over supply of linen and linen was not in a great demand. There were to many linen mills and not enough money to be made in the county. Stringer decided to leave Ireland and move to Canada. Stringer must of been financially secure because of the cost the ships passage to bring his family of 13 in the year of 1817, must of been substantial. In the Land Transaction Records for Lanark County. I found an entry relating to Stringer's arrival in Canada

After arriving in Quebec City they made their way to Prescott. According to what I have read on that period, It took roughly twelve days to reach Prescott from Montreal. I found another article about a minister from Scotland who landed the same day in Quebec but on another ship. He traveled by boat to Prescott then hired wagons drawn by oxen teams to take him to Brockville. He stated that it took two days to take him to Perth Military Settlement. I found it interesting that horses were not used for travel to inward areas of the wilderness and the reason why was of the lack of food. Oxen could forage in the woods and survive but horses were not suitable because of the lack of grass and hay. They were not adaptable to this wilderness environment.

Stringer and family stayed in Prescott a while then made their trek to their allotted property. I don't know how they traveled to their wilderness property but found an article about a road heading north. Stating

I don't know if they traveled all the way by road but the South Nation River flows by Stringers property so they could of used the water to access there property also?

They do not name another John Mcfadden and has documented proof of this John being a son through his other family members of John. John Mcfadden was born November 30, 1800 in Armagh Ireland. He married Hannah Snider born June 7, 1810, who was daughter of a Palatine Germaine Loyalist who came to Upper Canada from Vermont after the War of 1812. John first settled on Sw1/2 of lot 10, Concession 9, Edwardsburgh Twp, Grenville County. About 1825, he sold his original lot to his father Stringer, and moved to a lot near to and possibly owned by his father in law John Snider Lot 5, Concession 5, South Gower Twp. Census records for John Mcfadden and his family from 1834 to 1841 state that he lived at that lot with his family. In 1861 the census for Marborough Twp. Carelton county he was shown as a tenant on part of a lot owned by his daughter Sarah and her husband Lewis Pencier. In 1881 census of Burleigh Falls has John and Hannah in their 80's living in the household of their son Cyrus. In the 1891 census shows Hannah still living with her son but John is not listed. John died April 2,1887 while living with his son Cyrus in Burleigh Twp Peterborough county near Apsley. Hannah died also living with her son Cyrus in June 15, 1893. Their burial plots are not known.

3. William Mcfadden

William Mcfadden was born March 7, 1805, Armagh, Ireland. William married Susanah Wilson who was born in Ireland in 1817. William became a Wesleyan Methodist Minister in 1832. He belonged to the Brighton Circuit. Arnold White has supplied His obituary that was published in the Toronto Globe and Mail Dec. 10, 1885 Brampton, Dec. 8-- Rev. Wm. Mcfadden died here yesterday, in the 80th year of his age. For some time he had been invalid.; though being possessed of a strong will, he did not yield until he was compelled to-do so. The disease which had preyed upon him for several years was of such nature that it occasioned him intense suffering, which he endured with great fortitude and resignation.

Mr. Mcfadden entered the Wesleyan ministry in 1832(the Canadian Methodist Magazine said "He began labour as a Chairman's supply, at Agusta in 1832) The candidates for the ministry that year numbered ten, of whom the venerable Dr. Rose, of Toronto, is now the sole survivor. Mr. Mcfadden laboured eight years in the Ottawa Valley, after he was stationed in the town of Peterboro, and after serving full term there, he was moved to Picton, where he was elected Chairman of the District. He held this position somewhere about a dozen years. After having traveled with great acceptability for the space of forty years, he very reluctantly accepted the position of superannuated minister, but for some years afterwards he did good service in visiting of his old circuits and supplying vacancies. He was highly esteemed both by his brethren in the ministry and the people among whom he laboured. About the time of his superannuation his wife died, which was sever trial to him. Since then he has made is home with his son in law George Graham, County Treasure. Brampton was one of his last circuits so that his venerable form was well known to the inhabitants of that town of which he had been a resident, in all fifteen years. "

He leaves behind him three children, all of whom are married His only son William is Attorney for the County of Peel. His two daughters are married respectively to Mr. Graham, previously mentioned and Mr. John Jonas, Bowmanville.
